design guide

visual language for reports, dashboards, and ui · internal reference

philosophy

our design language exists to make information easy to absorb at a glance, beautiful to look at, and honest about what it's showing. every design choice serves the reader's time: the most important thing should be the most visually prominent, and anything secondary should recede without disappearing.

1
hierarchy is the design
the layout should tell the reader what to look at first, second, and third without labels like "summary" or "reference." glass weight, text size, padding, and position do this work. if you need to label something "important," the visual hierarchy isn't working.
2
informative, not prescriptive
present the landscape. name the patterns. show the data. let the reader draw their own conclusions. don't tell people what to do — give them what they need to decide. recommendations only when explicitly asked for.
3
every word earns its space
if information appears once, it shouldn't appear again in different words. shorten ruthlessly — concrete details over vague descriptions. if a chart already shows the data, the text around it should add context, not restate the numbers.
4
glass communicates weight
the glass morphism system isn't decoration — it encodes importance. stronger blur and opacity = primary content. lighter, more translucent = supporting detail. the reader's eye naturally follows the gradient from solid to soft.

typography

two typefaces. stix two text (serif) carries headlines, numbers, and section titles — anything the eye should land on first. manrope (sans-serif) handles body text, labels, descriptions — the supporting information. all text is lowercase unless a proper noun or acronym demands otherwise.

rules

do
all lowercase everywhere — headings, labels, body
serif for anything the eye should land on first
sans-serif for everything supporting
let text size alone create hierarchy (no bold + large + color stacking)
don't
title case, sentence case, or all caps (except rare acronyms)
mix serif into body paragraphs
go below 7px — that's the floor for legibility
use manrope for numbers or big stats — stix carries those

semantic usage

ink #1A1A2E — all primary text, headlines, item titles. never pure black.
accent blue #7B8FD4 — emphasis words in vision text, chart primary bars, accent lines, person names in team lists, validated/positive pills. the one "pop" color.
soft violet #C5C0E8 — secondary chart bars, negative/friction pills, de-emphasized groupings.
mid gray #8E8EA0 — descriptions, supporting text, labels, meta info, section headers in the reference layer. carries the bulk of the supporting text.
frost #F0F1F8 — chart bar tracks, pill backgrounds, subtle fills.
background #FAFAFD — page base with radial gradient overlays for depth.

background

the page is never flat white. layer radial gradients to create subtle ambient depth:

radial-gradient(ellipse at 25% 8%, rgba(123, 143, 212, 0.09), transparent 55%) — cool wash, upper left
radial-gradient(ellipse at 75% 85%, rgba(197, 192, 232, 0.07), transparent 50%) — warm wash, lower right
linear-gradient(160deg, #FAFAFD 0%, #EEF0FA 40%, #F5F5FA 70%, #FAFAFD 100%) — base

glass system

every container is a glass panel. the glass intensity communicates how important the content inside is. this is the primary tool for building visual hierarchy — not borders, not background colors, not headers. four tiers:

what makes it feel like glass

five properties working together. skip any one and it reads as a flat card, not a glass pane:

translucent fill — rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.3–0.58). never fully opaque.
backdrop blur — backdrop-filter: blur(16–32px) saturate(1.3–1.7). the blur makes the translucency visible.
specular top edge — inset 0 1.5px 0 rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.7). simulates light hitting the top edge of a pane.
luminous border — border: 1px solid rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.3–0.6). catches light along the full perimeter.
soft outer shadow — 0 4px 24px rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.05). lifts the pane off the background.

choosing a tier

glass-strong
scan layer
the thing the reader's eye hits first. north star cards, metric cards, primary data panels. the content someone needs to absorb in 30 seconds.
glass-tint
callout
sections that need emphasis with a semantic tint — feedback results, validated findings, key callouts. the blue tint signals "this is a distinct category of information."
glass
supporting
standard containers for data that matters but isn't primary. charts, blocker lists, stakeholder grids. the bulk of a page's structured content.
glass-receded
reference
information that's there to point at during discussion. stakeholder details, team perspectives, methodology notes. smaller text, thinner glass.

information hierarchy

every page — report, dashboard, or ui view — should have two layers. the reader should be able to identify which is which from the visual weight alone, never from a label.

scan layer

the content someone absorbs in 30 seconds if they walk in late. uses glass-strong and glass-tint, larger text (10–16px body, 14–28px headlines), more generous padding (14–18px). this layer sits at the top of the page and visually dominates. it should contain:

— the framing statement or vision (what is this about, why does it matter)
— the 2–4 most important structural facts (north stars, key metrics, scope)
— the 2–4 highest-signal data points (what's working, what's broken)

supporting layer

detail the team references during discussion. uses glass and glass-receded, smaller text (7.5–9px), tighter padding (11–14px). this layer occupies the lower portion of the page and visually recedes. the transition from scan to supporting should feel like the page is gently falling away — not like hitting a wall labeled "appendix."

ordering within each layer

content is ordered by importance to the reader, not by category. if feedback is more important than data, feedback sits above data. if stakeholder context is more important than team perspectives, it goes first. never default to a fixed template — ask "what does this audience need to see first?" and let that drive the layout.

components

metric / north star cards

glass-strong panels with a micro label (7–7.5px, 0.5 opacity ink, letterspaced), a serif headline (14–16px, 700 weight), and an optional description line (8–8.5px, mid gray). use these for the 2–4 most important structural facts on any page. grid them 1fr 1fr or repeat(3–4, 1fr) depending on count.

signal cards (validated / blockers)

glass-tint panels, always in pairs. one for positive signal, one for negative. top label in accent blue or soft violet at 7.5px letterspaced 700. items inside use 9–10px bold title + 8–9px gray description. these are for surfacing the most important findings from any data source — label them by source (e.g. "from ca feedback — 5 interviews") so the reader knows where the signal comes from.

bar charts

horizontal bars only. rounded tracks (border-radius: 50px) with inset shadow for depth. fill bars use gradient fills — primary: #8FA3DE → #7B8FD4, secondary: #D0CCF0 → #C5C0E8. labels on the left, count on the right in serif. keep labels descriptive and concrete — "hiring — co-founders, specialist engineers, sales hires" not just "hiring." bar width scales proportionally to the max value.

pills / tags

rounded capsules (border-radius: 50px) at 5.5–7px. positive: accent blue fill at 0.12 opacity, blue text, subtle blue border. negative: violet fill at 0.2, violet text. use sparingly — one pill per item max. pills should add a scannable semantic signal, not replace the label.

dividers

gradient lines, never solid: linear-gradient(90deg, transparent, #E0E0EA, transparent). 1px height. use between major sections in the scan layer. between scan and supporting, the glass weight change usually provides enough separation — add a divider only if the transition feels ambiguous.

team / perspective lists

bulleted lists inside a glass panel. each line: name in accent blue bold, — separator, then the perspective in ink. separated by 1px solid rgba(224, 224, 234, 0.3) dividers. keep each perspective to one sentence.

layout

page dimensions

reports: one letter page (8.5 × 11in), 0.45–0.5in top padding, 0.7in sides, 0.35–0.4in bottom. always print-to-pdf from the browser to preserve glass effects and google fonts. set @page { size: letter; margin: 0; } and handle padding on the body.

grids

2-column: 1fr 1fr — north stars, signal card pairs, side-by-side panels
2-column weighted: 7fr 3fr or 3fr 2fr — chart + context, data + key stats
3-column: repeat(3, 1fr) — reference panels, team perspectives
4-column: repeat(4, 1fr) — stakeholder cards, metric cards
gap: 12–14px throughout. never mix gap sizes on the same page.

writing

all text is lowercase. first person where a voice is needed. concise, concrete, and informative — name the thing, describe the thing, move on. the writing style mirrors the visual style: no decoration that doesn't earn its space.

do
"10 of 15 min spent typing. 3/5 cas raised this."
"hiring — co-founders, specialist engineers, sales hires"
"cas didn't ask for penelope. adoption has to be earned."
"data concentrated in weeks 2–3"
don't
"the data suggests that a significant proportion of time is spent on manual entry activities"
"team building and recruitment challenges"
"it is recommended that the team consider..."
"as we can see from the chart above..."

key rules

no redundancy. if a chart shows the data, don't restate it in prose. add context the chart can't carry — sample size, time window, caveats.
no prescriptions. present findings, not recommendations. "cas want weekly objectives" not "we should implement weekly objectives."
concrete over abstract. "3d printers, china supply chain delays" not "equipment and logistics challenges."
source your claims. label where findings come from — "from ca feedback — 5 interviews" or "30 of 74 residents have check-ins."
one sentence per perspective. team quotes, stakeholder descriptions, and finding descriptions should each fit in one line.

rendering

browser print-to-pdf (preferred)

open the html in chrome or safari and print to pdf. this preserves backdrop-filter glass effects, google fonts, and gradient backgrounds. set -webkit-print-color-adjust: exact and print-color-adjust: exact on html and body. no @media print overrides — the page should look identical on screen and paper.

fallback (legacy renderers)

if rendering with a tool that doesn't support backdrop-filter (wkhtmltopdf, older puppeteer), glass panels degrade to solid white with a border. this is acceptable but not preferred. for these environments, replace translucent backgrounds with solid #FFFFFF or #F8F8FC and drop the blur/saturate filters.

fonts

stix two text and manrope load from google fonts. in offline environments, they fall back to the system serif (liberation serif / times) and system sans-serif (liberation sans / arial). the design works with fallbacks but loses some of its character — when possible, ensure google fonts access or embed the woff2 files.

architectural background

cream base with three arched alcoves using border-radius: 50% 50% 0 0 on tall divs. subtle inset shadows on pillars for depth. side arches hide on mobile, center arch stretches full width. shared by both loading and results pages via <ArchBackground>.

column pedestal

replaces magazine-style gradient cards. each matched home is shown as a classical column with: golden ornamental capital (cornice, dentil band, lower molding), marble shaft with gold side trim, white recessed panel showing home name and location, gold base and feet. telescope icon floats above at an angle. hover: subtle scale + telescope rotation shift. data-testid=column-{slug} for test selectors.

golden letter overlay

replaces the white modal letter. golden gradient card (#E8C840 → #B89828) centered on screen with dark text. light backdrop (bg-black/20) instead of heavy dark overlay. contains personalized letter text, home signature, "close" link at bottom-left, and conditional "listen to our community architect" link at bottom-right (only if home has video_url). no inline video or question section — those are separate components.

video modal

white container, portrait aspect (9:16). X close button top-right. html5 video with controls and autoplay. z-index above golden letter (110/111) so it overlays on top. triggered from golden letter's video link.

questions floating card

replaces the full-screen all-questions modal. white card centered on page with very light backdrop (bg-black/10). "questions" title in serif. for each home with a question: home name label, question text, textarea. dark "submit" button closes the card. answers auto-save on change via the same mechanism as before.

golden button style

the "answer questions" button uses a golden gradient background: linear-gradient(135deg, #D4B040 0%, #C5A030 50%, #A88820 100%) with white text and a subtle gold shadow. used on the results page when questions remain unanswered.
